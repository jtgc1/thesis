\startchapter{Experimental Setup}
\label{chapter:Exp}

The spatial interpolation methods will be evaluated on two datasets. The first is a synthetic dataset produced via a simple physics-based soil simulation modelling the movement and biodegradation of hydrocarbons in the soil. This dataset will be used to evaluate the models in a variety of scenarios such as measurement noise, number of sensors, soil parameter noise and measurement dropout. Additionally, a subset of the simulated dataset will be used to tune the hyperparameters of the interpolation algorithms. The second dataset uses real measurements from a contaminated site in the Prairies of Canada. Due to the limited number of available data samples, the results here will simply be used to validate some of the results of the simulated dataset. First, we will examine the format of the physical dataset to establish the format and context for the creation of the simulated dataset. 


\section{Real-World Data}
\label{sec:real_data}

The real measurement dataset was collected from an active biostimulation remediation site on the Canadian Prairies. It consists of 27 data samples acquired from 11 measurement stake locations distributed across the contaminated area. Each stake location hosts a sensor array at 1.5~m, 2.5~m, and 3.5~m below the surface. These depths were chosen to capture the vertical extent of the PHC plume, which tends to migrate downward through the soil column under the influence of gravity and precipitation-driven leaching \cite{PHC_movement_source}.

At each depth, a Beer-Lambert sensor measures the absorbed light through the air to determine a PHC concentration at 30 minute intervals \cite{beerLambertSensor}. To reduce high-frequency measurement noise while preserving the underlying remediation dynamics, these raw readings are aggregated into 12-hour averages, yielding two observations per depth per day at each stake location. Each data sample in the dataset therefore consists of a time series of PHC concentration measurements at three depths from a single stake location, sampled at 12-hour increments. An example of a data sample can be seen below. 

 \begin{figure}[H]
 \centering
 \includegraphics[height = 4in]{figures/real_measurements.png}
 \caption{A sample from the real-world data set. The three plots depict the concentration of PHC as a \% of the atmosphere through a four-month time span at the three specified depths. The measurements are aggregated every 12 hours.}
 \label{fig:realMeas_intro}
 \end{figure}

The duration of the time series varies considerably across data samples, ranging from 30 to 250 days. This variability reflects the practical realities of field deployment. Sensors were installed and occasionally decommissioned at different times throughout the remediation process, and some units experienced extended periods of dropout due to hardware faults or adverse weather conditions. As a result, the dataset is irregular with stake locations distributed non-uniformly across the site and each location contributing multiple different lengths of observational records. These characteristics make the dataset representative of the kind of incomplete, heterogeneous data that spatial interpolation methods must contend with in real remediation settings.

A representative sample from the dataset are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:realMeas_intro}, illustrating the range of behaviours observed across stake locations. Some locations exhibit a clear monotonic decline in PHC concentration consistent with active biostimulation, while others show more complex dynamics including periods of stagnation or transient increases, likely attributable to soil heterogeneity or localized variations in nutrient distribution.


\section{Synthetic Data}
\label{sec:synthetic_data}

A synthetic data sample largely mirrors the real-world data format. Each sample contains temporal data at three depths of 1.5~m, 2.5~m, and 3.5~m with standard measurements every 12 hours. However, all of these properties can vary when testing the methods under a variety of conditions.

The synthetic data is generated from a physics-based simulation that incorporates diffusion, advection, and biodegradation of PHC concentration. The simulation operates in one dimension through the depth of the soil column to match the setup of the single-stake measurements. The cell size is fixed at 0.1~m per cell, and the total simulation depth is fixed at 4.5~m for all data points, providing a buffer between the lowest measurement depth and the boundary layer at the bottom of the simulation domain. The forward model governing contaminant concentration is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:soil_model}
\begin{aligned}
    \conc(\locvar,\, \timevar + \Delta\timevar) = \; & \conc(\locvar,\, \timevar) \\
    & + \advecCoeff(\locvar)
        && \text{(Advection)} \\
    & + \biodegCoeff(\locvar) \cdot \conc(\locvar,\, \timevar)
        && \text{(Biodegradation)} \\
    & + \diffCoeff(\locvar) \cdot \frac{\Delta\timevar}{\Delta\distvar^{2}}
      \Bigl[
        \conc(\locvar - \Delta\distvar,\, \timevar)
        - 2\conc(\locvar,\, \timevar)
        + \conc(\locvar + \Delta\distvar,\, \timevar)
      \Bigr]
        && \text{(Diffusion)}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\noindent
where $\conc(\locvar,\,\timevar)$ is the PHC concentration (\%) at depth $\locvar$~(m) and time $\timevar$~(hours). $\advecCoeff$~(\%\,hour$^{-1}$) is a simplified lateral advection term ranging from $\bigl(-10^{-3},\;10^{-3}\bigr)$~\%\,hour$^{-1}$ \cite{AdvectionCoeff}. $\biodegCoeff$~(hour$^{-1}$) is a proportional biodegradation rate representing the natural microbial breakdown of PHC in the soil, ranging from $\bigl(-10^{-3},\;10^{-3}\bigr)$~hour$^{-1}$ \cite{biodeg}. $\diffCoeff$~(m$^{2}$\,hour$^{-1}$) is the vertical diffusion coefficient governing PHC transport between neighbouring cells, ranging from $\bigl(10^{-10},\;10^{-6}\bigr)$~m$^{2}$\,hour$^{-1}$ \cite{diffcoeff, tempCoeff}. All three soil parameters are realized as vectors of $\nSimLoc$ values, one per cell in the soil column. Five pilot-point values are drawn from the random distributions shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sim_param_dist} and assigned to depths of 0, 1.1, 2.2, 3.3, and 4.5~m; all intermediate cell values are then obtained by interpolation.

Once the advection, biodegradation, and diffusion parameter vectors are constructed, the initial PHC concentration $\conc(\locvar,\,0)$ and boundary conditions must be specified for all \nSimLoc cells.

Four boundary condition scenarios are used in generating the simulated dataset. A PHC source at both the top and bottom of the soil column, a top-only source, a bottom-only source, and no sources. These cases represent the principal real-world configurations in which PHC originates from the surface, the water table, both, or neither \cite{hydrocarbon_in_soil_old,PHC_in_soil_newer}. Source boundary concentrations are drawn from a normal distribution with a mean of 3\% and a standard deviation of 0.5\%; all other initial PHC concentrations are drawn with a mean of 0.12\% and a standard deviation of 0.04\%, with a lower threshold of 0\%. These values are derived from the real-world data in section \ref{sec:real_data} and \cite{biodeg,oil_conc_soil}. As with the soil parameters, values are assigned at the pilot point locations and interpolated across all remaining \nSimLoc locations.

 \section{Hyperparameter Tuning}
 \label{sec:hyp_tuning}

 Below, each of the methods will undergo hyperparameter tuning to a training subset of 40 synthetic data samples.

 \subsection{Inverse Distance Weighting}
 \label{sec:idw_tuning}

 Hyperparameter tuning was not extensive for this method, since it is only the baseline algorithm. A grid search of $\idwpow$ and $\idwwin$ tested at 1, 2 and 4 and 1, 3 and 5 respectively was performed on the training synthetic data. The results can be found in figure \ref{fig:idw_hyperp}:

   \begin{figure}[!h]
 \centering
 \includegraphics[width = 4in]{figures/idw_gridsearch.png}
 \caption{Hyperparameter search for inverse distance weighting parameters $\idwpow$ and $\idwwin$. The colorbar represents MSE of the spatial approximation vs the synthetic ground truth. Measurement noise=0.02 which is quite large to properly evaluate the averaging window.}
 \label{fig:idw_hyperp}
 \end{figure} 

 These results are incredibly straight forward. As the power increases the MSE decreases. Larger powers of 4 were investigated, but as is visible in figure \ref{idw_example} that uses a power of 4, the resulting spatial distribution is already quite jumpy at a power of 4, so we decided to not go past it to keep the estimates it produces realistic. As for the window size, the results are not significant, as the window increased, the MSE remained relatively the same. Therefor, a measurement window of 3 will be used in the validation set to continue to partially use the temporal aspect of the data. 



\subsection{Kriging}
\label{sec:krig_tuning}

Minimal hyperparameter tuning was performed for Kriging, reflecting its role as a baseline method and its limited performance in preliminary testing. The model was evaluated on the 40-sample training set to determine the best combination of parametric fitting model and dissimilarity metric for constructing the experimental variogram. The fitting models considered were linear, exponential, Gaussian, spherical, and an automatic model selection, evaluated in a grid search against three dissimilarity metrics: temporal semi-variance, single semi-variance, and Pearson correlation, defined in Equations~\ref{eq:semVar2}, \ref{eq:semVar_more}, and \ref{eq:pearsonCorr}, respectively. The automatic model selection computes the fitting error for each of the other four parametric models against the empirical variogram and selects whichever achieves the lowest MSE.

\begin{figure}[!h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=5in]{figures/kriging_gridsearch.png}
    \caption{Grid search results for Kriging hyperparameters. The colorbar 
    represents the MSE of the spatial approximation relative to the synthetic 
    ground truth.}
    \label{fig:idw_hyperp}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:krig_example}, the experimental variograms in this problem contain limited amounts of data (2-6 points), causing the spherical and linear models to converge to the same line of best fit and thus produce identical scores. Consequently, the automatic model selection consistently selects one of these two models and likewise yields equivalent results. The Gaussian and exponential functions proved unable to accurately characterize the empirical variograms in this setting. Regarding the dissimilarity metric, temporal semi-variance substantially outperformed the alternatives, as it explicitly captures the temporal dimension of the data which the single semi-variance does not. The Pearson correlation did not adequately capture the dissimilarity metrics. Based on these results, the automatic model selection paired with temporal semi-variance (Equation~\ref{eq:semVar_more}) is adopted for all subsequent Kriging evaluations.



 \subsection{4D Variational Data Assimilation}
 Unlike the previous two distance-based interpolation methods, 4D-Var is the first of the methods that completely utilizes the temporal aspect of the data. As described in section \ref{sec:background_fdvar} 4D-Var utilizes the temporal data by matching a physics-based interpolation model to the provided measurements and once fit, the resulting PHC distribution is used as the spatial estimate. 4D-Var utilizes the adjoint model that provides the derivative of each soil parameter and initial condition relative to the final resulting spatial distribution to tune the parameters and initial conditions to reduce the error between simulation and measurements. 

 The first step, matching the temporal data can be seen in figure \ref{fig:fdvar_temporal_example}. To complete this matching step 4D-Var updates the parameters and initial conditions of the physics-based model by explicitly calculating the derivative of cost to each parameter and initial condition. This map is called the adjoint as described in section \ref{sec:background_fdvar}. This greatly reduces the number of model runs.

 \label{fig:fdvar_temporal_example}

   Priors for the initial conditions of PHC concentration are simply the first measurements given in the data sample at the given location and all other cells are interpolated. The boundary conditions/sources are set low to 0.1\% limit the methods from biasing towards large sources. The parameters $\advecCoeff, \biodegCoeff \& \diffCoeff$ are set to the middle of their range resulting in $0, 0, 1e^(-8)$ respectively. The middle option is a common prior when no other priors can be used. More advanced inference could be made to influence this assumption, however that remains out of scope.

 \subsection{GLM}

The only difference is the method of calculating the derivative of error relative to each parameter and initial condition. As described in \ref{sec:background_glm} the derivative is calculated by running the model with small perturbations in the value and recording the cost.
