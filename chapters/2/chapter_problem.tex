This chapter establishes the mathematical framework underlying the spatial interpolation problem studied in this thesis. Each method is situated within the context of petroleum hydrocarbon (PHC) transport and remediation. The main task is to estimate the spatial distribution of a petrochemical contaminant plume from a spatially sparse, noisy set of measurements collected across space and time. Each considered method approaches this task from a different theoretical perspective, ranging from purely geometric interpolation to physics-informed parameter estimation.

Measurements $\conc(\locvar,\timevar)$ are acquired at a location $\locvar$ and time $\timevar$. The variable $\locvar$ was used since our problem is reduced to one-dimension in depth and $\locvar$ is a common notation for depth. Each evaluated method produces an estimate $\hat{\conc}(\locvar_o,\timevar)$ at unmeasured depths $\locvar_o$, which is compared against available ground truth via the Mean Squared Error (MSE), $(\conc(\locvar_o,\timevar) - \hat{\conc}(\locvar_o,\timevar))^2$, at a given time $\timevar$.  In practice, a continuous spatial ground truth is only available when working with synthetic data. For real-world datasets, leave-$n$-out cross-validation is employed as a substitute. It is also worth noting that $\timevar$ typically corresponds to the current or most recent time step, as remediators are primarily concerned with the present-day plume distribution. 

\section{Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW)}
\label{sec:background_idw}

Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) is the simplest method evaluated in this thesis and serves as a baseline against which the more sophisticated approaches are benchmarked. It relies solely on the geometric relationship between measurement locations and the target estimation point, making no assumptions about the underlying physical process and only minimally exploiting the temporal structure of the data. Despite its simplicity, IDW provides an important reference point for understanding when temporal dynamics and physics-based constraints meaningfully improve estimation performance, and when the additional complexity of those approaches offers little advantage over a naive geometric approach.

Standard IDW computes a weighted average over all $\nSampLoc$ measurement locations at a given time $\timevar$. The weight of each measurement is relative to the inverse of the distance between the measurement depth and the target depth $\locvar_o$ raised to a user-specified power.

 \begin{equation}
 \label{eq:idw}
    \hat{conc}(\locvar_o,\timevar) = 
    \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\nSampLoc} w_i \cdot \conc(\locvar_i,\timevar)}
         {\sum_{i=1}^{\nSampLoc} w_i}, 
    \qquad w_i = \frac{1}{d(\locvar_o,\locvar_i)^p}
 \end{equation}
 
A minor modification is introduced to tailor IDW to the time-series element of this problem. Rather than using only the instantaneous measurement $\conc(\locvar_i,\timevar)$ at each location, the algorithm is supplied with the average of the most recent $\idwwin$ observations at that location. This windowed average serves to dampen measurement noise without fundamentally altering the character of the algorithm. In summary, IDW uses two hyperparameters $\idwpow$, the power of the distance relationship and $\idwwin$, the width of the temporal averaging window.






\section{Kriging}
\label{sec:background_kriging}

Kriging is a geostatistical interpolation method that shares the weighted-average structure of IDW but replaces the fixed power-law decay with an adaptive weighting scheme derived from the spatial covariance structure of the data, allowing it to capture a wider variety of spatial dependence patterns.

As with IDW, traditional Kriging treats each location independently, drawing on a single observation per site at time $\timevar$ \cite{kriging_overview}. The foundational quantity in Kriging is the semi-variance between a pair of measurement locations $\locvar_{i_1}$ and $\locvar_{i_2}$, defined as:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:semVar2}
   \gamma(\locvar_{i_1}, \locvar_{i_2}) 
   = \frac{1}{2} \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_1},\timevar) 
   - \conc(\locvar_{i_2},\timevar)\right)^2
\end{equation}

Computing this quantity for all pairs of measurement locations and plotting the results as a function of pairwise separation distance yields the empirical variogram, an example of which is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:kriging_semVar}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=4in]{figures/semVar.png}
    \caption{Example empirical variogram constructed from pairwise semi-variance values plotted against separation distance. Parametric model in red fit to the semi-variance values.}
    \label{fig:kriging_semVar}
\end{figure}

A parametric model is then fit to the empirical variogram. Common choices include spherical, circular, exponential, Gaussian, and linear models, each encoding a different assumption about how spatial correlation decays with distance \cite{kriging_how_to}. Model selection is typically a subjective judgment based on goodness-of-fit to the empirical data. Due to the large amounts of testing data, the "SciPy minimize" library finds a locally optimal set of parameters for each parametric model. The fitted model is then used to evaluate the expected semi-variance at any arbitrary separation distance, including distances corresponding to unmeasured locations. These predicted semi-variances are used to construct the Kriging weights, which replace the fixed power-law weights in Equation~\ref{eq:idw} with a set of weights that minimize estimation variance subject to an unbiasedness constraint.

Two modifications are made to this standard formulation to better exploit the temporal structure of the data. The first replaces the single-snapshot semi-variance of Equation~\ref{eq:semVar2} with an average over all $\nSampTime$ time steps at which both locations have concurrent observations:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:semVar_more}
    \gamma(\locvar_{i_1}, \locvar_{i_2}) 
    = \frac{1}{2\nSampTime} \sum_{j_1=1}^{\nSampTime} \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_1}, \timevar_{j_1}) 
    - \conc(\locvar_{i_2}, \timevar_{j_1})\right)^2
\end{equation}

This temporal semi-variance produces a more stable empirical variogram by pooling information across time rather than relying on a single snapshot. The second modification replaces the semi-variance entirely with the Pearson correlation computed across all $\nSampTime$ concurrent observations at $\locvar_{i_1}$ and $\locvar_{i_2}$:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:pearsonCorr}
    \rho(\locvar_{i_1}, \locvar_{i_2})
    = \frac{\sum_{j_1=1}^{\nSampTime} 
        \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_1}, \timevar_{j_1}) - \bar{\conc}(\locvar_{i_1})\right)
        \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_2}, \timevar_{j_1}) - \bar{\conc}(\locvar_{i_2})\right)}
    {\sqrt{\sum_{j_1=1}^{\nSampTime} 
        \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_1}, \timevar_{j_1}) - \bar{\conc}(\locvar_{i_1})\right)^2}
     \sqrt{\sum_{j_1=1}^{\nSampTime} 
        \left(\conc(\locvar_{i_2}, \timevar_{j_1}) - \bar{\conc}(\locvar_{i_2})\right)^2}}
\end{equation}

Rather than measuring the magnitude of differences between locations, Pearson correlation captures the degree to which two locations co-vary over time, providing a complementary characterization of spatial dependence. Single semi-variance, temporal semi-variance, and Pearson correlation are independent dissimilarity metrics populating the empirical variogram, and their relative performance is evaluated in Section~\ref{sec:krig_tuning}.




\section{Parameter Estimation} 

4D Variational Data Assimilation (4D-Var), the Gauss-Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm (GLM), and the Iterative Ensemble Smoother (IES) share a parameter estimation framework that distinguishes them from the distance-based approaches above. Rather than interpolating directly from observed concentrations, these methods optimize a physics-based model of PHC transport and remediation to match spatiotemporal measurements. Specifically, the parameter vector $(\param_0)$ is estimated so that the model's simulated output at the sensor locations $(\est)$ matches the available measurements $(\meas)$ as closely as possible. This is formalized through a state-space model consisting of a state evolution equation, an observation equation, a cost function, and a smoother update equation:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:enkf_M}
    \hat{\paramComp}^{(k)} = \forwardFunc(\hat{\param}_{0}^{(k)}, w_\timevar), 
    \qquad w_\timevar \sim \mathcal{N}_n(0, Q) 
\end{equation}
\vspace{-1em}
\begin{equation} 
   \label{eq:enkf_H}
    \est = \observationFunc(\hat{\paramComp}^{(k)}, v_\timevar), 
    \qquad v_\timevar \sim \mathcal{N}_{m_\timevar}(0, R)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:paramEst_costFunc}
   \mathcal{J} = \sum_{i=1}^{\nSampLoc}\sum_{t=1}^{\nSampTime}(\est(\locvar_i,t) - \meas(\locvar_i,t))^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:smoother_update}
    \hat{\param}_0^{(k+1)} \leftarrow \hat{\param}_0^{(k)} + \delta\mathcal{J}
\end{equation}

Here, $\hat{\param}_0^{(k)}$ denotes the $k^{th}$ estimate of the parameter vector and $\est$ denotes the predicted observation vector over all $\nSampLoc$ sensor locations and $\nSampTime$ time steps. The operator $\forwardFunc$ is the forward model, which propagates the initial condition $\hat{\param}_0$ forward in time to produce the full simulated trajectory $\hat{\paramComp}$. The operator $\observationFunc$ maps this trajectory to the observation space, producing $\est$. The terms $w_\timevar$ and $v_\timevar$ represent zero-mean Gaussian process and observation noise, with covariance matrices $Q$ and $R$, respectively. 

Equation \ref{eq:paramEst_costFunc} measures the discrepancy between the estimated observations $\est$ and the measurements $\meas$. Finally, Equation \ref{eq:smoother_update} denotes the smoothing update, where $\hat{\param}_0^{(k+1)}$ is the updated parameter vector produced by the method-specific update term $\delta\mathcal{J}$.

For the present problem, the parameter vector $\hat{\param}_0$ contains the initial PHC concentration field $\hat{\conc}_0$ along with the soil process and boundary condition parameters. Although the latter do not evolve in time, they are included so that the smoother update can simultaneously refine both the concentration field and the underlying physical parameters. In practice, $\hat{\conc}_0$ and the physics parameters are not explicitly defined at every cell in the domain but rather at a sparse set of locations known as pilot points. The full concentration field is then interpolated from these values. The number of pilot points is therefore a key hyperparameter controlling the resolution of the parameterization. It is worth noting that the composition of $\hat{\param}_0$ is strongly problem-dependent. In numerical weather prediction it typically represents only the physical state of the system, whereas in hydrology and subsurface transport it may encompass physical parameters, initial conditions, or both. Additionally, the observation vector $\est$ spans both space and time, yielding a two-dimensional array of size $(\nSampLoc\times\nSampTime)$ that is flattened into a one-dimensional vector for computational convenience.

Once the model has been adequately calibrated, or a maximum number of iterations $(\numIterations)$ has been reached, the concentration field $(\hat{\conc}_\nSampTime)$ is extracted from the final simulated trajectory $\hat{\paramComp}^\numIterations$  at time step $\nSampTime$. This serves as the method's best estimate of the true spatial distribution.

This parameter estimation approach can offer a significant advantage over purely geometric methods. Physical constraints such as advection, diffusion, and biodegradation are implicitly embedded in the estimate, allowing the model to extrapolate into unobserved regions of the domain in a physically consistent manner. 4D-Var, GLM, and IES operate within this state-space framework, minimizing $\mathcal{J}$ by iteratively applying the smoother update in Eq.~\ref{eq:smoother_update}. The key distinction between them lies in how each computes the correction term $\delta\mathcal{J}$ which is discussed in the following sections. 




\subsection{4D Variational Data Assimilation (4D-Var)}
\label{sec:background_fdvar}

4D Variational Data Assimilation, commonly referred to as 4D-Var, is a deterministic optimization method that seeks to find the initial condition $\hat{\param}_0$ that minimizes the discrepancy between model predictions and observations across a defined assimilation window $[0, T]$ \cite{fdvar_og}. It has been the standard approach in operational numerical weather prediction for decades, but is less common in soil remediation practice due to its emphasis on refining initial conditions rather than stationary model parameters\cite{da_overview}.

In addition to the general cost function in Equation \ref{eq:paramEst_costFunc}, the 4D-Var cost function augments the data misfit in Equation~\ref{eq:paramEst_costFunc}. Using a regularization term that penalizes departures from a prior estimate $\param^b$ of the initial condition, weighted by the background error covariance matrix $B$:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:4dvar_cost}
   \mathcal{J}(\hat{\param}_0) = \frac{1}{2}(\hat{\param}_0 - \param^b)^\top 
   B^{-1}(\hat{\param}_0 - \param^b)
   + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=1}^{\nSampTime}
   \left(\est - \meas_\timevar\right)^\top R^{-1}
   \left(\est - \meas_\timevar\right)
\end{equation}

The first term encodes prior knowledge about the plausible range of parameter values and acts as a regularizer that prevents overfitting to the observed data. The second term is the observation misfit, accumulated over all assimilation times and weighted by the inverse of the observation error covariance $R$.

Minimizing $\mathcal{J}$ requires the gradient with respect to the initial condition $\nabla_{\hat{\param}_0}\mathcal{J}$, which is computed efficiently via the adjoint of the forward model $\forwardFunc$. The adjoint equations propagate the observation residuals backward through time, yielding the exact gradient of the cost function at a computational cost comparable to a single forward model integration, regardless of the dimension of $\hat{\param}_0$. This property makes 4D-Var well suited to high-dimensional problems\cite{fdvar_details}.

Given the gradient $\nabla_{\hat{\param}_0}\mathcal{J}$, any gradient-based optimizer such as L-BFGS, truncated Newton method or Adam can produce the update steps $\delta\mathcal{J}$ at each iteration. Updates proceeds until the gradient norm falls below a specified tolerance or the maximum number of forward model evaluations $(\numIterations)$ is reached. The evaluated hyperparameters of the 4D-Var model include $(\numIterations)$, the number of iterations and $\npp$ the number of pilot points used in the parameter vector. They are evaluated in section \ref{sec:fdvar_tuning}.

\subsection{Gauss-Levenberg-Marquardt (GLM)}
\label{sec:background_glm}

The Gauss-Levenberg-Marquardt (GLM) algorithm, as implemented in the PEST software suite \cite{pestManual}, is a gradient-based nonlinear least-squares method. It belongs to the family of trust-region methods and can be understood as a regularized form of the classical Gauss-Newton algorithm. GLM has been widely applied to groundwater model calibration and contaminant transport inverse problems, where it remains a practical workhorse owing to its relative computational efficiency and well-understood convergence properties \cite{glm_example}.

At each iteration, GLM linearizes the forward model $\forwardFunc$ around the current estimate of the initial condition $\hat{\param}_0^{(k)}$ by computing the Jacobian matrix:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:jacobian}
   J_{ij} = \frac{\partial \est(\locvar_i, \timevar_i)}{\partial \hat{\param}_{0,j}}
   \Bigg|_{\hat{\param}_0^{(k)}}
\end{equation}

where the $(i,j)$ entry gives the sensitivity of the model output at depth $\locvar_i$ and time $t$ to a perturbation in the $j$-th component of $\hat{\param}_0$. This Jacobian is estimated by finite differences, requiring one forward model run per parameter \cite{glm_details}.

The smoother update in Equation~\ref{eq:smoother_update} is then applied with the correction $\delta\mathcal{J}$ computed as:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:glm_update}
   \delta\mathcal{J} = \left(J^\top R^{-1} J + \lambda I\right)^{-1} J^\top 
   R^{-1}\left(\meas - \est\right)
\end{equation}

The scalar $\lambda \geq 0$ is the Marquardt parameter, which controls the interpolation between a pure Gauss-Newton step ($\lambda = 0$, fast convergence near the optimum) and a steepest-descent step ($\lambda \to \infty$, robust far from the optimum). PEST adapts $\lambda$ automatically across iterations using a line-search heuristic. For example, $\lambda$ is increased when $\mathcal{J}$ fails to decrease, and reduced when sufficient progress is made.

The hyperparameters for GLM are the same as 4D-Var. $\numIterations$ the number of iterations and $\npp$ the number of pilot points used in the model. The results of these hyperparameters are examined in section \ref{sec:glm_tuning}.



\subsection{PEST-IES}
\label{sec:background_ies}

The Iterative Ensemble Smoother (IES), takes a fundamentally different approach from both 4D-Var and GLM by representing uncertainty in the parameter vector through an ensemble of candidate solutions rather than seeking a single point estimate \cite{pestIES}. This ensemble-based perspective is closely related to the Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) and its smoothing variants, but is formulated as an iterative batch smoother that assimilates all observations simultaneously rather than sequentially.

An ensemble of $\nEnsMember$ parameter realizations $\{\param^{(j)}_0\}_{j=1}^{\nEnsMember}$ is drawn from the prior distribution $\mathcal{N}(\param^b, B)$, where $\param^b$ is the prior mean and $B$ is the prior covariance. Each realization is propagated through the forward model $\forwardFunc$ to produce a corresponding ensemble of simulated observations:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:ies_ensemble}
   \est^{j} = \observationFunc(\forwardFunc(\hat{\param}^{j}_0)), 
   \qquad j = 1, \ldots, \nEnsMember
\end{equation}

The ensemble is then updated using a form of the ensemble Kalman update, in which the empirical cross-covariance between the parameter ensemble and the simulated observation ensemble is used in place of the exact Jacobian:

\begin{equation}
   \label{eq:ies_update}
   \param^{j,k+1} \leftarrow \param^{j,k} 
   + C_{\param \est}\left(C_{\est \est} + R\right)^{-1}
   \left(\meas^{(j)} - \est^{(j)}\right)
\end{equation}

where $C_{\param \est}$ is the ensemble cross-covariance between parameters and simulated observations and $C_{\est \est}$ is the ensemble covariance of the simulated observations. $\meas^{(j)}$ is a perturbed observation vector drawn from $\mathcal{N}(\meas, R)$ to account for observation uncertainty. The second term of Eq. \ref{eq:ies_update} represents the correction term from Eq. \ref{eq:paramEst_costFunc}. Unlike the standard EnKF, IES applies this update iteratively, re-running the forward model after each update step and recalculating the ensemble covariances. This iteration corrects for the linearization error inherent in the single-step Kalman update and allows the ensemble to converge toward the posterior distribution even in the presence of moderate nonlinearity.

A key practical advantage of IES over GLM is that it does not require explicit computation of a Jacobian matrix; the ensemble covariances implicitly provide the sensitivity information needed for the update. This makes it particularly attractive for high-dimensional problems where finite-difference Jacobian estimation would demand a prohibitive number of forward model evaluations. However, the size of the parameter vector in the current problem never exceeds 35 parameters. Therefore, no computational gains are made by avoiding the computation of a Jacobian matrix.

Furthermore, the ensemble representation naturally quantifies parametric uncertainty. The spread of the posterior ensemble can provide valuable information for decision-making in the remediation context. The tuned hyperparameters for the model are $\numIterations$ the number of update iterations and $\nEnsMember$ the ensemble size of the method. These are evaluated in section \ref{sec:ies_tuning}.
