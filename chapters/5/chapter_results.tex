\section{Synthetic Data}
The synthetic data will be broken into each of the variations supplied above and each of the methods will be compared under the assumptions. To begin in this section we will evaluate the methods on the base case and establish the comparison format. 

To establish the facts the base case operates with 0 measurement error, 0 soil parameter error, 2 month long measurement times with 12 hour sampling frequency and no measurement dropout.

The results from this plot immediately become clear about the trajectory of the upcoming results. The parameter estimation methods that utilize the physics-based model are able to estimate the spatial distribution of the PHC plume much better than the purely distance-based methods. The mean MSE values for the parameter estimation values is not the standout, however the median lines on the box plots is. The parameter estimation methods are able to put significantly more data samples in the low error range, however they do still suffer from the over guessing and then the larger errors appear. Another area where the parameter estimation algorithms shine is the lower interquartile ranges which once again like the median exemplifies the lower MSE that the parameter estimation methods can produce with the physics-informed analysis. 

Within the parameter estimation algorithms, 4D-Var performs the worst, followed by GLM and then IES performs the best. This is highly correlated with the number of model runs completed. Due to the high ensemble size of IES, it produces a significantly higher number of model runs than the other methods. Likewise, since GLM utilizes model runs to produce the gradient, it also produces a significantly larger number of model runs in comparison to 4D-Var.

\section{Real-World Data}


Now that the datasets and methods are clear and described we can begin comparing them in clear and described tests. First, all of the methods will undergo stress tests with modifications to the simulated dataset. These stress tests will include measurement noise, number of sensors, soil parameter noise and sensor dropout. All the methods IDW, Kriging, 4D-Var, GLM and IES will be compared on 12 tests to evaluate and compare their potential performance on the real data. They will be primarily evaluated on their ability to predict the simulated spatial distribution of PHC in a vertical column given temporal measurements from a small amount of sensors located in that same column. Additionally, the time these methods take to perform the approximation will be recorded and compared. Finally, with these tests complete the methods will be evaluated on real data from a contaminated site using leave-1-out cross validation since a spatial ground truth is no longer available. 

To begin, all five methods are evaluated on the base case. The base case includes 12 hour measurement frequency, three sensors located at 1.5, 2.5 and 3.5~m, no measurement noise, no soil parameter noise and no sensor dropout. In this case, we see the beginnings of a trend that we will see throughout the remainder of the results. IDW and Kriging are outperformed by the parameter estimation methods. Kriging is significantly unable to capture any meaningful relationship between the parameters due to the lack of spatial data supplied to it. Inverse distance weighting is the best distance-based interpolator. It can accurately capture data samples with no source due to all the spatial evaluation points being rather close together. However, when faced with a source or two sources, its performance drops, due to the sensors not always capturing these high concentration sources.
