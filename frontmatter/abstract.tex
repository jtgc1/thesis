\section*{\large Abstract}
 
Canada faces a growing number of abandoned oil wells and petrochemical contamination sites. Effective in situ biostimulation remediation depends on accurately mapping petroleum hydrocarbon (PHC) plume distributions from sparse sensor measurements. This thesis evaluates five spatial interpolation methods for this task: Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW), Kriging, 4D-Variational Data Assimilation (4D-Var), the Gauss-Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm (GLM), and the Iterative Ensemble Smoother (IES). They are benchmarked on a physics-based synthetic dataset and validated on real sensor data from the Canadian Prairies.
 
Physics-based parameter estimation methods (4D-Var, GLM, IES) substantially outperform distance-based methods across nearly all conditions tested, including measurement noise, sensor dropout, and reduced sensor counts. IES achieves the best overall accuracy and additionally provides an ensemble-based uncertainty estimate, but at approximately $21\times$ the runtime of 4D-Var. Kriging offers no advantage over IDW given the variogram sparsity inherent to the single-stake measurement format. Where computational cost is acceptable, IES is the recommended method for PHC plume reconstruction at active remediation sites.
